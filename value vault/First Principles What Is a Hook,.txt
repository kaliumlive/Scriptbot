First Principles: What Is a Hook, Really?

Before we talk about tactics, let's define what we're actually doing.

**A hook is not about grabbing attention. It's about introducing a pattern interrupt.**

When someone scrolls, their brain is in a low-energy state. They're not actively searching. They're passively consuming. Your hook needs to create a **cognitive interrupt**—a moment where their pattern recognition system says, *"Wait, that's different."*

This happens in one of three ways:

1. **Contradiction** → You challenge something they believe to be true.
2. **Specificity** → You say something so precise it feels personal.
3. **Identity Alignment** → You speak directly to their current pain or aspiration.

When you understand this, you stop writing hooks that "sound good" and start writing hooks that **force a reaction**.

---

We’re entering a shift in content.

For years, content was about value. Tips, hacks, information. Now, it’s about *how* that value is delivered. Story is becoming the differentiator.

In a world where AI tools make it possible for anyone to look like anyone, say anything, and generate any visual they want, *presentation* is everything. Information alone isn’t rare anymore. Perspective is.

The real skill people are searching for now isn’t how to use the tools. It’s how to use them to guide attention, create emotion, and carry someone through an idea in a way that feels human and intentional.

---

**So how do you do that?**

There are a few core ways to present information that actually pulls people in instead of pushing information at them.

---

### 1. Think Bigger

When you approach a topic, zoom out before you zoom in.

Instead of starting with specifics, ask yourself how the idea can be framed broadly enough that almost anyone can see themselves in it, while still being specific enough to spark curiosity about where the story is going.

The goal is not to explain everything upfront. It’s to invite the viewer into a question.

A great example of this is how certain creators title and structure their content. The titles feel universal. Anyone could click them. But once you’re inside, the creator slowly walks you through the idea, using that overarching question as a guide. You’re not being lectured. You’re discovering the answer alongside them.

That sense of movement is what keeps people watching.

---

### 2. Be Insanely Personal

Tie yourself to the idea.

The strongest stories don’t just teach something. They reveal something about the person telling them. There’s a reason the quote exists: *“The most personal is the most universal.”*

When someone speaks honestly about small, overlooked moments, the quiet thoughts, the things people rarely articulate, it gives others permission to feel seen. Those details create connection far more than polished explanations ever could.

Often, the content we resonate with most isn’t the loud or impressive stuff. It’s the moments that touch something in us we didn’t even realize we were carrying.

When you speak about the small things with intention, people don’t just understand your message. They recognize themselves in it.

# How to Tell Better Stories

## **My Short-Form Storytelling Guide**

**How to keep viewers watching, feeling, and curious 🧐**

---

### **Rule #1: The Hook Is Everything**

If the hook doesn’t hit, the rest of the story is pointless.

A strong hook must do at least one of these in the first 1–2 seconds:

- Create curiosity
- Introduce tension
- Make a bold promise
- Challenge a belief
- Present an outcome people want to see

**Bad hook:**

“Today I want to talk about storytelling.”

**Good hook:**

“Most creators suck at hooks because they give all the sauce in the first 2 seconds.”

If the hook doesn’t force the viewer to think *“wait… what?”*, start over.

---

## **Rule #2: Stories Must Move Forward**

The fastest way to lose retention is by **stalling the story**.

That’s where the **ABT Method** comes in.

---

## **The ABT Method (And, But, Therefore)**

This is one of the most powerful storytelling frameworks ever created, and it’s perfect for short-form.

### **AND Set the Context**

This is what’s happening. Keep it tight.

> “The point of your hook is to build curiosity and call out your audience”
> 

Use **AND** to establish context.

---

### **BUT – Introduce Conflict**

This is the most important word in storytelling.

> “But if you don’t deliver on your promise…”
> 

Conflict creates **curiosity**. Curiosity creates retention.

No **BUT** = no reason to keep watching.

If you remove this part and the story still works, your story is weak.

---

### **THEREFORE (or “so”) – Deliver the Shift**

This is the decision, insight, or solution that moves the story forward.

> “So, if you really want people to watch until the end, make a bold statement and deliver the value at the end of the video. ”
> 

This is where:

- Lessons are learned
- Decisions are made
- Value is delivered

---

## **Rule #3: Curiosity Beats Information**

The best scripts don’t dump information.

They **withhold it strategically**.

Your job is not to explain everything.

Your job is to make the viewer **want the next sentence**.

Ways to build curiosity:

- Open loops
- Unanswered questions
- Delayed explanations
- Outcome-first statements

**Instead of:**

“Here are 3 storytelling tips.”

**Try:**

“By the end of this, you’ll know why most creators lose viewers halfway through.”

---

## **Rule #4: Every Line Must Earn the Next**

In short-form, every sentence must justify the next cut.

Ask yourself after every line:

> “Does this make someone want to keep watching?”
> 

If the answer is no, cut it.

No filler.

No warm-ups.

No context without tension.

---

## **Simple Short-Form Story Formula**

Use this for almost anything:

1. **Hook** – Create curiosity or tension immediately
2. **AND** – Set the scene
3. **BUT** – Introduce the problem
4. **THEREFORE** – Deliver insight, shift, or solution
5. **Close the loop** – Reward the viewer

---

## **Final Reminder**

Editing doesn’t save bad storytelling.

Visuals don’t replace curiosity.

And consistency doesn’t fix weak hooks.

If the hook hits, people stay.

If the story moves, people care.

If curiosity is high, the algorithm follows.
